About Us

A new voice for progress

We believe the most important questions about progress aren't being asked by the people closest to them.

Why we exist

In 2019, Patrick Collison and Tyler Cowen called for a new science of progress — one that would study why progress happens and how to make it happen faster. Since then, a vibrant ecosystem has emerged: the Roots of Progress Institute, the Institute for Progress, Works in Progress, and a growing community of researchers and builders.

But this ecosystem has a blind spot. Almost all of its institutional energy is concentrated on the rich world. The conferences are in Berkeley. The policy work targets Washington. The implicit subject of "progress" is the American frontier.

This is a mistake — not just morally, but intellectually. The developing world is where the most interesting natural experiments in progress are happening right now. Why did Rwanda's digital ID system succeed while others failed? What drove Vietnam's manufacturing boom? How did M-Pesa transform Kenya's financial system? These aren't footnotes to the progress story — they're the main text.

Vox Nova exists to build the research infrastructure and talent pipeline that this field needs. We run fast-cycle research programs — sprints, studios, and fellowships — that produce rigorous work without requiring a PhD or a visa to San Francisco.

Output over credentials

We don't care where you went to school. We care what you can produce. Every level of our pipeline selects on demonstrated research ability.

Speed with rigor

Academic timelines are too slow for the pace of change in the developing world. We produce working papers in weeks, not years — without cutting corners on methodology.

Global South as producer

We don't study the developing world from a distance. We build research capacity within it. Our hub model ensures the community is rooted locally.

Actionable knowledge

Every research output should be usable — by a policymaker, an entrepreneur, or another researcher. Theory that doesn't connect to practice stays on the shelf.
