## 1. The Gap

The progress studies movement — launched by Collison and Cowen's 2019 Atlantic article and now anchored by Roots of Progress, Institute for Progress, Works in Progress, and others — asks a powerful question: *why does progress happen, and how do we make it happen faster?*

But almost all of its institutional energy is concentrated on the rich world. The conferences are in Berkeley. The policy work targets the US federal government. The fellowship pipelines feed into San Francisco startups and DC think tanks. The implicit subject of "progress" is the American frontier.

Meanwhile, the places where progress is most urgent — Eastern Europe, Latin America, the Middle East, Southeast Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa — have no equivalent infrastructure. There is no talent pipeline for researchers who want to study *why Lagos grows while Kinshasa stagnates*, or *what made the Baltic states' digital transformation work*, or *how Vietnam's manufacturing ascent differs from Bangladesh's*.

Development economics exists, of course. But it operates within legacy academic institutions — slow, credentialist, siloed. The Apart Research model has shown that a lightweight, sprint-based, globally distributed organization can produce serious research output (22 peer-reviewed publications, 3,500+ participants, papers at ICLR and NeurIPS) at a fraction of the cost and time of traditional programs.

**This org applies the Apart Research model — sprints, studios, fellowships — to progress studies for the developing world.**

---

## 2. Mission & Theory of Change

**Mission:** Accelerate progress in the developing world by building a global research and talent pipeline focused on understanding what drives — and what blocks — economic, institutional, and technological advancement in low- and middle-income countries.

**Theory of change:**

1. **Identify talent globally** through low-barrier research sprints that let people demonstrate capability without quitting their jobs or getting a visa
2. **Develop that talent** through structured programs (studio → fellowship) that produce real research output
3. **Generate actionable knowledge** — not just academic papers, but policy briefs, case studies, and tools that practitioners and policymakers can use
4. **Build a community** of progress-oriented researchers, entrepreneurs, and policymakers across the developing world who know each other and collaborate
5. **Shift the conversation** — make "progress studies for the developing world" a recognized field with its own questions, methods, and career paths

## 3. Organizational Structure

### 3.1 The Pipeline (adapted from Apart Research)

```
SPRINTS (48-hour research hackathons)
  │  200+ participants per sprint, global, online-first
  │  Low barrier: anyone with relevant skills can join
  │  Produce: pilot experiments, data analyses, policy briefs
  │
  ▼
STUDIO (4-week research accelerator)
  │  15-25 participants selected from sprint outputs
  │  Mentorship from domain experts
  │  Produce: research proposals, working papers
  │
  ▼
FELLOWSHIP (3-6 month intensive research program)
  │  5-10 fellows per cohort
  │  Compute resources, funding, publication support
  │  Produce: peer-reviewed papers, policy reports, datasets
  │
  ▼
ALUMNI NETWORK + PLACEMENT
    Career support, ongoing collaboration,
    policy advisory roles, founding support
```

### 3.2 Program Details

#### Research Sprints

**Format:** 48-hour hybrid events (online participation globally + optional in-person hubs in partner cities)

**Frequency:** One sprint every 6-8 weeks (6-8 per year)

**Structure:**
- **Friday evening:** Kick-off, problem framing, team formation
- **Saturday:** Research & build (mentors available on call)
- **Sunday:** Submissions due by 4pm, presentations and judging in the evening
- **Monday:** Winners announced, best teams invited to Studio

**What makes a sprint topic?** Each sprint focuses on a specific question at the intersection of progress and the developing world. Example topics:

- *Why do some post-Soviet economies grow while others stagnate?*
- *Digital public infrastructure: what can countries learn from India's Aadhaar/UPI stack?*
- *Industrial policy in Southeast Asia: causal evidence from Vietnam, Indonesia, Thailand*
- *The institutional roots of the Latin American middle-income trap*
- *Energy transitions in Sub-Saharan Africa: leapfrogging vs. incremental paths*
- *Brain drain or brain circulation? Diaspora effects on home-country progress*
- *Special economic zones: when do they work and when are they rent-seeking?*
- *Why did the Baltics succeed at digital government? Lessons for other post-communist states*
- *Urbanization and productivity in Africa: Lagos, Nairobi, Kigali compared*
- *What actually works in education reform? Evidence from Chile, Rwanda, and Vietnam*

**Outputs:** Each sprint produces 20-40 pilot projects. Best ones are published on the org's website as working notes. Top teams get prizes (small cash + Studio invitation).

**In-person hubs:** For each sprint, we partner with local universities, coworking spaces, or research orgs in 3-5 cities to host watch parties / co-working sessions. This builds local community without requiring everyone to travel. Target hub cities: Tbilisi, Bogota, Nairobi, Ho Chi Minh City, Beirut, Kigali, Tashkent, Lagos, Sao Paulo, Amman.

#### Studio Program

**Format:** 4-week part-time program (10-15 hrs/week commitment)

**Cohort size:** 15-25 participants per cohort, running 3-4 times per year

**Structure:**
- Week 1: Research question refinement + literature review
- Week 2: Methodology workshop + data identification
- Week 3: Analysis and drafting
- Week 4: Presentation to expert panel + feedback

**Mentors:** Recruited from development economics, political economy, economic history, and policy. Mix of academics and practitioners (think: someone from the Central Bank of Rwanda AND someone from MIT's development lab).

**Outputs:** Research proposals ready for fellowship-level development, or standalone working papers.

#### Research Fellowship

**Format:** 3-6 month intensive, fully remote with optional residency weeks

**Cohort size:** 5-10 fellows per cohort, 2 cohorts per year

**What fellows get:**
- Monthly stipend (calibrated to cost of living — not one-size-fits-all)
- Compute and data resources
- 1:1 mentorship from a senior researcher
- Publication support (editing, submission strategy)
- Travel funding for 1-2 in-person residency weeks with the cohort

**What fellows produce:**
- A peer-reviewed paper OR a substantive policy report
- A public dataset or tool (where applicable)
- A presentation at one of our partner events

**Selection:** Based entirely on Studio/Sprint output quality. No credential requirements — we care about what you've produced, not where you went to school.

#### Annual Conference

One flagship event per year, rotating between developing-world cities. Hybrid format. Brings together sprint alumni, fellows, policymakers, and funders. Modeled loosely on the Roots of Progress Conference but focused entirely on the developing world.

## 4. Research Agenda

The org doesn't prescribe research conclusions, but it does frame the questions. Five thematic pillars:

### Pillar 1: Institutions & Governance
Why do some countries build effective institutions while others don't? What makes institutional reform stick? What's the role of state capacity, legal systems, and bureaucratic quality?

*Key questions:* post-conflict institution building, anti-corruption mechanisms that actually work, decentralization outcomes, digital governance adoption.

### Pillar 2: Technology & Industrial Development
How do countries move up the value chain? What drives manufacturing growth, tech sector development, and agricultural modernization? When does industrial policy work?

*Key questions:* special economic zones, technology transfer, local innovation ecosystems, leapfrogging vs. sequential development, the role of diaspora entrepreneurs.

### Pillar 3: Human Capital & Demographics
What builds productive human capital? How do education systems, health systems, and migration patterns affect long-run growth? What happens when countries go through the demographic transition?

*Key questions:* education reform that works, brain drain/circulation, fertility transition effects, skills mismatches, the role of universities.

### Pillar 4: Finance & Infrastructure
How do countries fund development? What's the role of domestic capital markets, international finance, remittances, and aid? What infrastructure investments have the highest returns?

*Key questions:* mobile money and financial inclusion, infrastructure prioritization, debt sustainability, the role of development finance institutions, crypto/fintech in developing economies.

### Pillar 5: Culture, Ideas & Political Economy
Why do some societies embrace change while others resist it? What's the role of ideas, narratives, and cultural attitudes toward innovation, risk, and entrepreneurship?

*Key questions:* the role of diaspora communities, cultural attitudes toward business and technology, how reform coalitions form, media ecosystems and information quality.

## 5. What Makes This Different

| | Traditional dev econ | Progress studies (existing) | This org |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Geography** | Global South as subject | Rich world as subject | Global South as both subject AND producer of research |
| **Talent model** | PhD pipeline (5-7 years) | Fellowships & blogs | Sprint → Studio → Fellowship (weeks to months) |
| **Speed** | Slow (journal timelines) | Medium (blogs, reports) | Fast (sprint outputs in 48hrs, papers in 6 months) |
| **Access** | Credentialist | Network-based | Output-based (show what you can do) |
| **Orientation** | Descriptive / evaluative | Pro-progress, forward-looking | Pro-progress, developing-world-specific |
| **Outputs** | Academic papers | Policy memos, blog posts | Papers + policy briefs + datasets + tools |
| **Community** | Conference-based, fragmented | US/UK concentrated | Distributed, with hubs in target regions |

## 10. Risks & Mitigations

| Risk | Likelihood | Mitigation |
|---|---|---|
| **Can't attract enough sprint participants** | Medium | Partner with existing communities (EA groups, dev econ networks, university clubs in target countries). Start with topics that have built-in audiences |
| **Quality of sprint outputs too low** | Medium | Invest in good problem framing, provide starter datasets, recruit strong mentors. Quality improves as community matures |
| **Funding doesn't materialize** | High (pre-seed reality) | Start extremely lean. Founders work unpaid initially. First sprint can run on <$5k. Build evidence before asking for large grants |
| **Confused with development aid / charity** | Medium | Be clear about identity: this is a *research and talent* org, not an aid org. Progress studies framing helps |
| **Brain drain criticism** | Low-Medium | Emphasize that the pipeline is designed to build capacity *in* the developing world. Hub model keeps community rooted locally. Stipends calibrated to local cost of living |
| **Too broad geographically** | Medium | Start with 2-3 focus regions where you have connections. Expand as community grows organically |
| **Apart Research already expanding into this** | Low | Apart focuses on AI safety specifically. Different domain, complementary approach. Could even partner |
