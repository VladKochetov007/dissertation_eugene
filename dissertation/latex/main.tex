\chapter{Policy Methods in Reinforcement Learning}
\label{ch:policy-methods}

This chapter introduces the mathematical framework of reinforcement learning that underpins the rest of the dissertation. We begin with the Markov Decision Process, which formalises how an agent interacts with an uncertain environment, and then develop the key objects---policies, value functions, and returns---needed to state optimality conditions. In the second half of the chapter we turn to \emph{policy gradient methods}, a family of algorithms that directly parametrise and optimise the agent's policy. The material in this chapter follows \cite{sutton2018rl}, primarily Chapters~3 and~13; all notation introduced here will be extended to the multi-agent setting in later chapters.

\section{Markov Decision Processes}
\label{sec:mdp}

A \emph{Markov Decision Process} (MDP) provides a principled abstraction for sequential decision-making under uncertainty. An MDP is a tuple
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mdp-tuple}
  M = \langle \mathcal{S},\, \mathcal{A},\, \mathcal{P},\, \mathcal{R},\, \gamma \rangle,
\end{equation}
where the components are defined as follows.

\begin{definition}[MDP components]\label{def:mdp}

\begin{enumerate}[label=(\roman*)]
  \item $\mathcal{S}$ is a finite set of \emph{states}.
  \item $\mathcal{A}$ is a finite set of \emph{actions} available to an agent.\footnote{More generally one may allow state-dependent action sets $\mathcal{A}(s)$; we use a single set for notational simplicity.}
  \item $\mathcal{P}\colon \mathcal{S}\times\mathcal{A}\times\mathcal{S} \to [0,1]$ is the \emph{transition function}, where $\mathcal{P}(s'\mid s,a) = \Pr\{S_{t+1}=s' \mid S_t=s,\,A_t=a\}$.
  \item $\mathcal{R}\colon \mathcal{S}\times\mathcal{A}\times\mathcal{S} \to \mathbb{R}$ is the \emph{reward function}; the agent receives $r_{t+1} = \mathcal{R}(s_t,a_t,s_{t+1})$ upon transitioning from $s_t$ to~$s_{t+1}$ via action~$a_t$.
  \item $\gamma \in [0,1)$ is the \emph{discount factor}, which controls the trade-off between immediate and future rewards.
\end{enumerate}
\end{definition}

The defining property of an MDP is the \emph{Markov property}: the distribution over future states depends on the past only through the current state. Formally,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:markov-property}
  \Pr\{S_{t+1}=s',\, R_{t+1}=r \mid S_t,\,A_t\} = \Pr\{S_{t+1}=s',\, R_{t+1}=r \mid S_0,A_0,R_1,\ldots,S_t,A_t\}.
\end{equation}
This memoryless structure is what makes the MDP framework tractable: the agent need not maintain a record of its entire history in order to act optimally.

\section{Policies}
\label{sec:policies}

A \emph{policy} $\pi$ specifies how the agent selects actions. In the most general (stochastic) case, a policy is a mapping from states to probability distributions over actions:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:policy-def}
  \pi(a\mid s) = \Pr\{A_t = a \mid S_t = s\}, \qquad a \in \mathcal{A},\; s \in \mathcal{S}.
\end{equation}
A policy is said to be \emph{deterministic} if, for every state~$s$, the distribution $\pi(\cdot\mid s)$ places all its mass on a single action. In the policy gradient framework developed in Section~\ref{sec:policy-gradient-methods}, we will consider parametrised stochastic policies $\pi(a\mid s, \bm{\theta})$ where $\bm{\theta}\in\mathbb{R}^d$ is a vector of learnable parameters.

\section{Returns and Value Functions}
\label{sec:returns-values}

The agent's objective is to maximise cumulative reward. The \emph{discounted return} from time step~$t$ is defined as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:return}
  G_t = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \gamma^k\, R_{t+k+1}\,.
\end{equation}
When the interaction naturally terminates after a finite number of steps (the \emph{episodic} case), the sum is finite and runs up to the terminal time step~$T$; in the \emph{continuing} case the discount factor $\gamma<1$ ensures convergence.

The expected return from a given state, under a fixed policy, is captured by the \emph{state-value function}:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:state-value}
  v_\pi(s) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\!\left[ G_t \mid S_t = s \right] = \mathbb{E}_\pi\!\left[ \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \gamma^k\, R_{t+k+1} \;\middle|\; S_t = s \right].
\end{equation}
The subscript~$\pi$ on the expectation indicates that actions are chosen according to~$\pi$. If we additionally condition on the first action taken, we obtain the \emph{action-value function} (or \emph{Q-function}):
\begin{equation}\label{eq:action-value}
  q_\pi(s,a) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\!\left[ G_t \mid S_t = s,\, A_t = a \right].
\end{equation}

The two value functions are related by a simple marginalisation over the policy:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:v-from-q}
  v_\pi(s) = \sum_{a\in\mathcal{A}} \pi(a\mid s)\, q_\pi(s,a).
\end{equation}
This identity will reappear repeatedly when we compute policy gradients, both in the single-agent setting and in the multi-agent extensions of later chapters.

\subsection{The Bellman Equations}
\label{subsec:bellman}

The value functions satisfy recursive relationships known as the \emph{Bellman equations}. For the state-value function,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bellman-v}
  v_\pi(s) = \sum_{a\in\mathcal{A}} \pi(a\mid s) \sum_{s'\in\mathcal{S}} \mathcal{P}(s'\mid s,a) \bigl[\mathcal{R}(s,a,s') + \gamma\, v_\pi(s')\bigr],
\end{equation}
and for the action-value function,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:bellman-q}
  q_\pi(s,a) = \sum_{s'\in\mathcal{S}} \mathcal{P}(s'\mid s,a) \bigl[\mathcal{R}(s,a,s') + \gamma \sum_{a'\in\mathcal{A}} \pi(a'\mid s')\, q_\pi(s',a')\bigr].
\end{equation}

These equations express a consistency condition: the value of a state must equal the immediate reward plus the discounted value of successor states, averaged over the randomness in the policy and the environment. They form the basis of many solution methods in reinforcement learning, including dynamic programming, temporal-difference learning, and Monte Carlo estimation~\cite{sutton2018rl}.

\subsection{Optimal Policies and Optimal Value Functions}
\label{subsec:optimal}

A policy $\pi^*$ is \emph{optimal} if $v_{\pi^*}(s) \ge v_\pi(s)$ for all $s\in\mathcal{S}$ and all policies~$\pi$. The corresponding \emph{optimal value functions} are
\begin{equation}\label{eq:optimal-v-q}
  v_*(s) = \max_\pi v_\pi(s), \qquad q_*(s,a) = \max_\pi q_\pi(s,a).
\end{equation}
The optimal value functions satisfy the \emph{Bellman optimality equations}, obtained by replacing the average over the policy in \eqref{eq:bellman-v}--\eqref{eq:bellman-q} with a maximum over actions. A fundamental result of MDP theory guarantees that at least one deterministic optimal policy always exists for finite MDPs~\cite{sutton2018rl}.

While value-based methods (such as Q-learning) seek the optimal value function and derive a policy from it, the policy gradient approach takes a different route: it parametrises the policy directly and optimises it via gradient ascent. We develop this idea next.

\section{Policy Gradient Methods}
\label{sec:policy-gradient-methods}

In many problems of interest---particularly those with large or continuous state spaces, or where the optimal policy is naturally stochastic---it is advantageous to parametrise the policy directly. We write
\begin{equation}\label{eq:parametrised-policy}
  \pi(a \mid s,\, \bm{\theta}) = \Pr\{A_t = a \mid S_t = s,\, \bm{\theta}_t = \bm{\theta}\},
\end{equation}
where $\bm{\theta}\in\mathbb{R}^d$ is a parameter vector. The goal is to find parameters that maximise a scalar \emph{performance measure} $J(\bm{\theta})$. The agent updates its parameters by approximate gradient ascent:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:gradient-ascent}
  \bm{\theta}_{t+1} = \bm{\theta}_t + \alpha\,\widehat{\nabla J(\bm{\theta}_t)},
\end{equation}
where $\alpha>0$ is a step size and $\widehat{\nabla J(\bm{\theta}_t)}$ is a stochastic estimate of the gradient $\nabla_{\bm{\theta}} J(\bm{\theta})$.

\subsection{Advantages of the Policy Gradient Approach}
\label{subsec:pg-advantages}

Several properties make policy gradient methods attractive compared to value-based alternatives.

First, a parametrised policy can smoothly interpolate between stochastic and near-deterministic behaviour. As the parameters evolve, the action probabilities change \emph{continuously}, in contrast to value-based methods with $\varepsilon$-greedy action selection, where small changes in estimated values can cause abrupt switches in the selected action. This continuity is precisely what enables gradient-based optimisation and provides stronger convergence guarantees.

Second, in problems where the optimal policy is inherently stochastic (for instance, in partially observable environments or in game-theoretic settings), policy gradient methods can represent and learn such policies directly, whereas value-based methods with greedy action selection cannot.

Third, policy parametrisation offers a natural mechanism for injecting domain knowledge or structural priors. For example, one can constrain the policy to lie in a particular function class (e.g.\ a neural network with a specific architecture) that reflects prior beliefs about the structure of good policies.

These advantages come at a cost: policy gradient estimates are typically of higher variance than value-based updates, and careful variance reduction techniques (such as baselines and actor-critic methods) are often necessary in practice.

\subsection{The Performance Measure}
\label{subsec:performance-measure}

In the \emph{episodic} setting, a natural performance measure is the expected return from a designated start state~$s_0$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:performance-episodic}
  J(\bm{\theta}) = v_{\pi_{\bm{\theta}}}(s_0) = \mathbb{E}_{\pi_{\bm{\theta}}}\!\left[G_0 \mid S_0 = s_0\right].
\end{equation}
More generally, if episodes can start from different states according to some distribution, one defines $J(\bm{\theta}) = \sum_s d_0(s)\, v_{\pi_{\bm{\theta}}}(s)$, where $d_0$ is the initial state distribution.

In the \emph{continuing} setting, one instead uses the average reward: $J(\bm{\theta}) = \lim_{T\to\infty}\frac{1}{T}\sum_{t=0}^{T-1} \mathbb{E}[R_{t+1}\mid \pi_{\bm{\theta}}]$. The results we present below apply to both settings with minor notational adjustments; we focus on the episodic case, as it is the setting used in \cite{kim2021policygradientalgorithmlearning} and in the cooperative steering game of later chapters.

\subsection{The On-Policy Distribution}
\label{subsec:on-policy-dist}

A key quantity in the theory of policy gradients is the \emph{on-policy state distribution} (or \emph{stationary distribution}) under policy~$\pi$, denoted $\mu_\pi(s)$. Informally, $\mu_\pi(s)$ measures how much time the agent spends in state~$s$ when following~$\pi$.

In the episodic case, let $\eta(s)$ denote the expected number of (discounted) visits to state~$s$ per episode:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:eta}
  \eta(s) = \sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \gamma^t\, \Pr\{S_t = s \mid S_0 = s_0,\, \pi\}.
\end{equation}
The on-policy distribution is then defined as the normalised version:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:mu-pi}
  \mu_\pi(s) = \frac{\eta(s)}{\sum_{s'}\eta(s')}\,.
\end{equation}
This distribution plays a crucial role in the Policy Gradient Theorem, as it determines which states contribute most to the gradient of the performance measure. Importantly, $\mu_\pi$ depends on the policy $\pi_{\bm{\theta}}$, and hence on the parameters~$\bm{\theta}$.

\section{The Policy Gradient Theorem}
\label{sec:pgt}

The central theoretical result for policy gradient methods is the \emph{Policy Gradient Theorem}, which provides an exact expression for $\nabla_{\bm{\theta}} J(\bm{\theta})$ that, remarkably, does not require differentiating the state distribution~$\mu_\pi$.

\begin{theorem}[Policy Gradient Theorem {\cite[Ch.~13]{sutton2018rl}}]
\label{thm:pgt}
For any differentiable parametrised policy $\pi(a\mid s,\bm{\theta})$, the gradient of the performance measure satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pgt}
  \nabla_{\bm{\theta}} J(\bm{\theta}) \propto \sum_{s\in\mathcal{S}} \mu_\pi(s) \sum_{a\in\mathcal{A}} q_\pi(s,a)\, \nabla_{\bm{\theta}}\,\pi(a\mid s,\bm{\theta}),
\end{equation}
where $\mu_\pi$ is the on-policy distribution \eqref{eq:mu-pi} and $q_\pi$ is the action-value function \eqref{eq:action-value}.
\end{theorem}

The significance of this result cannot be overstated. Naively, computing $\nabla J$ would require differentiating $\mu_\pi(s)$ with respect to~$\bm{\theta}$, since the state distribution itself depends on the policy. This derivative is extremely difficult to compute in practice, as it involves the long-run dynamics of the Markov chain induced by the policy. The Policy Gradient Theorem shows that $\nabla J$ can nonetheless be written as an expectation involving only $q_\pi$ and $\nabla_{\bm{\theta}}\pi$---both of which are far more accessible.

We present the full proof of Theorem~\ref{thm:pgt} in the next chapter, where we work through the derivation in detail and discuss the assumptions involved. The multi-agent generalisation of this result, due to \cite{kim2021policygradientalgorithmlearning}, is the subject of Chapter~6.

\subsection{The Score Function and the REINFORCE Estimator}
\label{subsec:reinforce}

A standard identity from probability theory, often called the \emph{log-derivative trick} or \emph{score function trick}, allows us to rewrite the gradient as an expectation suitable for Monte Carlo estimation. For any positive $\pi(a\mid s,\bm{\theta})$,
\begin{equation}\label{eq:log-trick}
  \nabla_{\bm{\theta}}\,\pi(a\mid s,\bm{\theta}) = \pi(a\mid s,\bm{\theta})\, \nabla_{\bm{\theta}}\,\ln\pi(a\mid s,\bm{\theta}).
\end{equation}
Substituting \eqref{eq:log-trick} into \eqref{eq:pgt} yields
\begin{equation}\label{eq:pgt-expectation}
  \nabla_{\bm{\theta}} J(\bm{\theta}) \propto \mathbb{E}_{s\sim\mu_\pi,\, a\sim\pi} \bigl[ q_\pi(s,a)\, \nabla_{\bm{\theta}}\,\ln\pi(a\mid s,\bm{\theta}) \bigr].
\end{equation}

This expectation can be estimated from sample trajectories, leading to the \textsc{reinforce} algorithm~\cite{sutton2018rl}. Given a trajectory $\tau = (s_0, a_0, r_1, s_1, a_1, r_2, \ldots)$ sampled under the current policy, the update at each time step~$t$ is
\begin{equation}\label{eq:reinforce-update}
  \bm{\theta}_{t+1} = \bm{\theta}_t + \alpha\, G_t\, \nabla_{\bm{\theta}}\, \ln\pi(a_t\mid s_t,\bm{\theta}_t),
\end{equation}
where $G_t$ is the observed return from time~$t$. This replaces the unknown $q_\pi(s_t,a_t)$ with the sample return~$G_t$, which is an unbiased estimate.

\subsection{Baselines and Variance Reduction}
\label{subsec:baselines}

While the \textsc{reinforce} estimator is unbiased, it can have very high variance, making learning slow. A standard technique is to subtract a \emph{baseline} $b(s)$ from the return:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:baseline}
  \bm{\theta}_{t+1} = \bm{\theta}_t + \alpha\, \bigl(G_t - b(s_t)\bigr)\, \nabla_{\bm{\theta}}\,\ln\pi(a_t\mid s_t,\bm{\theta}_t).
\end{equation}
Crucially, the baseline does not introduce bias into the gradient estimate, provided $b(s)$ does not depend on the action. This follows from the fact that $\sum_a \nabla_{\bm{\theta}}\pi(a\mid s,\bm{\theta}) = \nabla_{\bm{\theta}} 1 = 0$, so $\mathbb{E}_{a\sim\pi}[b(s)\,\nabla_{\bm{\theta}}\ln\pi(a\mid s,\bm{\theta})] = 0$.

A natural choice of baseline is an estimate of the state-value function $\hat{v}(s,\bm{w})$, parametrised by a separate weight vector $\bm{w}\in\mathbb{R}^m$. This leads to \emph{actor-critic} methods, in which the ``actor'' (the policy $\pi_{\bm{\theta}}$) and the ``critic'' (the value estimate $\hat{v}_{\bm{w}}$) are updated simultaneously.

\subsection{Actor-Critic Methods}
\label{subsec:actor-critic}

In an actor-critic architecture, the update to the policy parameters takes the form
\begin{equation}\label{eq:actor-critic-update}
  \bm{\theta}_{t+1} = \bm{\theta}_t + \alpha\, \delta_t\, \nabla_{\bm{\theta}}\,\ln\pi(a_t\mid s_t,\bm{\theta}_t),
\end{equation}
where $\delta_t = r_{t+1} + \gamma\,\hat{v}(s_{t+1},\bm{w}) - \hat{v}(s_t,\bm{w})$ is the \emph{temporal-difference (TD) error}. The TD error serves as a one-step estimate of the \emph{advantage function} $A_\pi(s,a) = q_\pi(s,a) - v_\pi(s)$, which measures how much better action~$a$ is compared to the average behaviour under~$\pi$ in state~$s$.

The critic parameters~$\bm{w}$ are updated to minimise the mean squared error of the value estimate, typically via semi-gradient TD:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:critic-update}
  \bm{w}_{t+1} = \bm{w}_t + \beta\, \delta_t\, \nabla_{\bm{w}}\,\hat{v}(s_t,\bm{w}_t),
\end{equation}
where $\beta>0$ is a separate step size.

Actor-critic methods combine the strengths of both policy gradient and value-based approaches: the policy parametrisation provides the representational flexibility discussed in Section~\ref{subsec:pg-advantages}, while the learned value function reduces the variance of the gradient estimates. This combination is essential in practice, and forms the backbone of many modern reinforcement learning algorithms.

\section{Trajectories and Episodes}
\label{sec:trajectories}

Before proceeding to the proof of the Policy Gradient Theorem, we formalise the concept of a \emph{trajectory}, which will be central to the multi-agent setting of later chapters. A trajectory (or \emph{episode}) is a complete sequence of states, actions, and rewards generated by an agent interacting with the environment under some policy~$\pi$:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:trajectory}
  \tau = (s_0,\, a_0,\, r_1,\, s_1,\, a_1,\, r_2,\, \ldots,\, s_{H-1},\, a_{H-1},\, r_H),
\end{equation}
where $H$ is the \emph{episode horizon}. The probability of a trajectory under policy~$\pi$ and transition dynamics~$\mathcal{P}$ factorises as
\begin{equation}\label{eq:trajectory-prob}
  p(\tau \mid \pi) = d_0(s_0)\,\prod_{t=0}^{H-1} \pi(a_t\mid s_t)\,\mathcal{P}(s_{t+1}\mid s_t,a_t),
\end{equation}
where $d_0$ is the initial state distribution. The discounted return along a trajectory is $G(\tau) = \sum_{t=0}^{H-1}\gamma^t r_{t+1}$, and the performance measure can be written as $J(\bm{\theta}) = \mathbb{E}_{\tau\sim p(\cdot\mid\pi_{\bm{\theta}})}[G(\tau)]$.

This trajectory-level perspective is particularly natural in the multi-agent setting of \cite{kim2021policygradientalgorithmlearning}, where each agent collects $K$~trajectories before updating its policy, and the meta-learning objective is defined over sequences of such trajectory batches.

\label{sec:ch3-discussion}
